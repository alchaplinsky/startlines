01 April, 2012
Something referred to as Medium was built. They had real code, a working product and a desire to ship something. At that time there were 3 designers: Dustin Senos, Leigh Taylor and Dann Petty. Dustin was doing double-duty as a developer and designer while Dann and Leigh focused on design.
12 August, 2012
Medium, a blog-publishing platform, was founded by Twitter co-founders Evan Williams and Biz Stone. The platform has evolved into a hybrid of non-professional contributions and professional, paid contributions, an example of social journalism.
15 November, 2012
The platform software provides a full WYSIWYG user interface when editing online, with various options for formatting provided as the user runs over text. Once an entry is posted, it can be recommended and shared by other people, in a similar manner to Twitter.
01 January, 2013
The platform uses the system of recommendations to up vote the best articles and stories. The Tag system divides the stories into different categories to let the audiences choose. Users can create a new account using an email address or a Twitter, Facebook, or Google account.
01 January, 2013
Medium has been described as “a cross between Tumblr and Pinterest.” Like Tumblr, it relies on artfully constructed templates for its structural power; like Pinterest, it’s designed to be image-heavy. But those surface issues, while interesting, are less consequential than the underlying structure of Medium, which upends much of how we think about personal publishing online.
01 April, 2013
Medium has relaunched Matter, the British tech publication, as the focus of its publishing efforts, admitting for the first time that it is a publisher as well as a publishing platform. The original version of Matter, a British science and technology journalism startup has been quietly closed after the relaunch. Its former editor, Bobbie Johnson, previously a Guardian reporter, remains involved with the project and still works at Medium, but Matter will now be edited by Mark Lotto, formerly a senior editor at GQ.

Medium goes after Tumblr with changes. Medium is turning the tables and moving into Tumblr’s territory instead with a series of changes that encourage writers to share stories of any length – even shorter posts and incomplete thoughts. Changes: no title required, simple formatting options, one click publishing into your stream.

Other “collections” have been merged, according to various reports, including Matter, which is effectively no longer considered its own site.
